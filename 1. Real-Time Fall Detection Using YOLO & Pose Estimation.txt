1. Abstract

Falls are one of the leading causes of serious injuries among elderly individuals and patients under continuous care, making early detection and rapid response critically important. Traditional fall detection systems often rely on wearable sensors, manual monitoring, or rule-based video analysis techniques, which suffer from limitations such as user discomfort, false alarms, and lack of real-time intelligence. This project presents a real-time fall detection system using YOLO-based object detection combined with pose-aware visual understanding to automatically identify fall events from images or video frames.

The proposed system leverages deep learning and computer vision techniques to analyze human posture and spatial orientation within a scene. By using a trained YOLO model, the system detects human presence and classifies fall-related postures with high accuracy. Bounding box detection, confidence scoring, and spatial analysis enable the system to differentiate between normal activities such as walking or sitting and abnormal fall scenarios, reducing false positives.

To ensure real-time usability, the system is deployed as a web-based application using the Flask framework. Users can upload images or frames captured from surveillance cameras, and the system instantly processes them to determine whether a fall has occurred. Detected results are visually displayed with bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores, making the output interpretable even for non-technical users.

This project demonstrates the effectiveness of deep learning-driven fall detection in healthcare and smart surveillance environments. By combining real-time inference, visual explainability, and scalable deployment, the system provides a reliable and automated solution that can significantly improve emergency response times and enhance safety for vulnerable populations.

6. Limitations of This Project

Although the proposed Real-Time Fall Detection Using YOLO and Pose Estimation system demonstrates strong performance and practical usability, certain limitations exist due to technical, environmental, and resource-related constraints. These limitations highlight areas where the system may face challenges in real-world deployment and indicate opportunities for future improvement.

Dependence on Camera Quality and Placement

The accuracy of fall detection heavily depends on the quality, resolution, and positioning of the camera. Poor lighting conditions, low-resolution cameras, or incorrect camera angles can reduce detection accuracy. If the subject is partially visible or captured from an extreme angle, the model may fail to detect body posture correctly.

Occlusion and Overlapping Objects

When the human body is partially occluded by furniture, walls, or other individuals, the system may struggle to correctly identify posture and movement. Occlusions can interfere with YOLO’s bounding box detection and pose estimation, leading to missed detections or incorrect classifications.

Limited Performance in Crowded Environments

In environments with multiple people, overlapping bounding boxes and similar movements may confuse the detection model. The system is optimized primarily for single-person fall detection scenarios, and performance may degrade in crowded or highly dynamic settings.

Dataset Dependency

The performance of the model is closely tied to the diversity and quality of the training dataset. If the dataset lacks sufficient variations in fall types, body orientations, clothing styles, or environmental settings, the model’s ability to generalize to unseen scenarios may be limited.

Computational Resource Requirements

Real-time detection using YOLO requires sufficient computational resources, particularly GPU acceleration for optimal performance. On low-end systems or edge devices without hardware acceleration, inference speed may decrease, affecting real-time responsiveness.

Static Image-Based Analysis

The current implementation primarily focuses on frame-level detection rather than continuous temporal analysis. Without analyzing motion across consecutive frames, some complex fall patterns may not be captured accurately, especially slow or gradual falls.

False Positives in Similar Postures

Activities such as lying down intentionally, exercising, or stretching may resemble fall postures and trigger false alerts. While pose estimation helps reduce such errors, complete elimination of false positives remains a challenge.

Environmental Variability

Changes in background, shadows, reflections, or camera vibrations can impact detection performance. The system may require recalibration or retraining when deployed in significantly different environments.

12. Algorithm Used

The proposed fall detection system employs a deep learning–based object detection algorithm designed for real-time performance and high accuracy. The algorithm focuses on identifying humans in images and classifying their posture to determine fall events. By combining fast detection with confidence-based evaluation, the system ensures reliable and efficient fall recognition suitable for safety-critical environments.

YOLO (You Only Look Once) is used as the core detection algorithm due to its single-stage architecture. Unlike traditional object detection models that perform region proposal and classification in separate steps, YOLO processes the entire image in one forward pass. This allows the model to predict bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores simultaneously, enabling real-time inference without compromising accuracy.

During detection, the input image is divided into a grid, and each grid cell predicts bounding boxes with associated confidence values. These predictions are filtered using a confidence threshold to remove weak detections. For each valid detection, the algorithm identifies the class label and bounding box coordinates, which are then used to analyze posture and spatial orientation. The relative shape and alignment of the bounding box play a key role in distinguishing falls from upright postures.

To enhance reliability, the algorithm applies non-maximum suppression to eliminate overlapping detections and retain only the most accurate bounding box per individual. This ensures clear visualization and prevents duplicate detections. The final output includes labeled bounding boxes and confidence scores, which are rendered on the image and displayed to the user. This algorithmic approach balances speed, accuracy, and explainability, making it highly effective for real-time fall detection systems.

14. Methodology

The methodology of the Real-Time Fall Detection Using YOLO and Pose Estimation system follows a structured and systematic approach to ensure accurate detection, real-time performance, and user-friendly interaction. The overall process begins with input acquisition and progresses through deep learning–based analysis, visualization, and result presentation. Each stage is designed to minimize latency while maintaining high detection reliability.

The first stage involves image acquisition through the web-based interface. Users upload an image captured from a surveillance camera or monitoring device. Once uploaded, the image is stored securely in the server directory and prepared for processing. Preprocessing steps include reading the image using OpenCV and ensuring proper resolution and format compatibility for the YOLO model.

In the next stage, the YOLO model processes the image to detect human presence. The model identifies bounding boxes around detected individuals and assigns class labels with confidence scores. These predictions are filtered using a predefined confidence threshold to eliminate weak detections. Non-maximum suppression is applied to remove overlapping bounding boxes, ensuring accurate localization.

After detection, posture analysis is performed by evaluating bounding box orientation and spatial alignment. This helps differentiate fall scenarios from upright or seated postures. Detected results are then visualized by drawing bounding boxes and labels directly on the image. The annotated image is saved and displayed to the user along with detection status. This end-to-end methodology ensures fast, reliable, and explainable fall detection suitable for real-world deployment.

17. Source Code Explanation

The source code of the Real-Time Fall Detection system is organized into multiple functional stages, each carefully designed to handle image input, human detection, posture analysis, and result visualization. The system uses Flask as the web framework, Ultralytics YOLO for object detection, and OpenCV for image handling.

The system creates directories for storing uploaded images and YOLO output graphs, ensuring that all data is organized and easily accessible. The YOLO model is loaded from a pre-trained weight file, which provides the backbone for human detection in the images.

Upon receiving an HTTP POST request with an image, the system saves the uploaded image using a unique filename generated by the UUID module. This prevents file conflicts when multiple users interact with the system simultaneously. OpenCV reads the saved image into memory, preparing it for detection. The YOLO model is then invoked to predict human presence, returning bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores. These outputs allow the system to localize individuals and provide information about posture and potential fall events.

For each detected human, the system draws a bounding box on the image and annotates it with the label and confidence score. This visualization step, powered by OpenCV, provides clear feedback to users and enhances explainability. The processed image is saved, and its path is sent to the frontend for display. Additionally, all detected labels are aggregated and presented to the user, giving a comprehensive overview of the detection results. This structured workflow ensures seamless integration of real-time detection, explainable outputs, and user-friendly interaction, forming a complete end-to-end fall detection solution.

Precision and Recall Analysis

Precision measures the proportion of correctly identified falls among all predicted falls, while recall measures the proportion of actual falls correctly detected by the system. The YOLO-based detection combined with posture evaluation achieves high precision and recall, indicating that both false alarms and missed detections are minimized. These metrics are critical for ensuring trust in a safety-critical system, where incorrect alerts can lead to unnecessary panic or ignored emergencies.

Visualizations such as precision and recall curves provide insight into the model’s performance across confidence thresholds. By analyzing these curves, developers can adjust detection parameters to optimize the trade-off between precision and recall. This level of analysis ensures that the system remains effective in real-world conditions where fall events are rare but high-impact, balancing safety with operational efficiency.

F1-Score Evaluation

The F1-score, which combines precision and recall into a single metric, reflects the overall reliability of the system. The model consistently achieves high F1-scores, demonstrating its ability to maintain both high sensitivity and specificity. This metric is particularly important in fall detection applications, as it ensures that the system can detect true fall events without over-alerting caregivers.

The F1-score curve generated during model evaluation illustrates the system’s stability across various confidence thresholds and environmental variations.
